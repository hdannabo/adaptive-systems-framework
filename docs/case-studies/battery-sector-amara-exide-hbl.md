# Indian Battery Sector: ASF Case Study
## Amara Raja Energy & Mobility · Exide Industries · HBL Engineering

**Framework:** Adaptive Systems Framework (ASF)  
**Analysis Date:** June 14, 2026  
**Data Coverage:** FY25 (April 2024 – March 2025) and available FY26 disclosures  
**Evidence Standard:** All claims traceable to named public sources. Undisclosed data marked explicitly.

---

## 1. Strategic Context

India's battery industry is undergoing a structural split. The lead-acid battery (LAB) market — worth approximately ₹25,000 crore domestically — is mature, capital-efficient, and dominated by Exide and Amara Raja. Simultaneously, India's EV transition is creating demand for lithium-ion cells that no Indian company currently manufactures at commercial scale.

The strategic question the ASF asks of all three companies is: **Can this organization build the required capability fast enough to achieve its strategic objective before conditions change?**

For Amara Raja and Exide, the required capability is commercial lithium-ion cell manufacturing before Chinese and Korean cell imports lock in OEM procurement relationships. For HBL Engineering, the required capability is sustained execution of government-mandated Kavach rail safety orders while diversifying away from telecom batteries that are in structural decline.

These are fundamentally different races. The ASF scores them on the same dimensions but their strategic risk profiles are not comparable.

## 2. Company-by-Company Analysis

### 2.1 Amara Raja Energy & Mobility

**Public identity:** India's second-largest lead-acid battery company by revenue. Strategic pivot to become a diversified energy technology company by FY30.

**FY25 financials (consolidated, audited):**
- Revenue: ₹12,846 crore (+9.7% YoY)  
- EBITDA: ₹1,617 crore (margin 12.6%, compressed from 14.2%)  
- PAT: ₹945 crore (+1.1%)  
- Credit rating: CRISIL AA+/Stable; CRISIL A1+

Source: NSE investor presentation; Autocar Professional, May 2025.

**Margin compression:** The 160 basis point EBITDA margin drop in FY25 was driven by antimony and lead alloy cost increases. Antimony (a grid hardener in lead-acid batteries) surged approximately 5x YoY due to Chinese export restrictions. This is not company-specific — Exide faced the same pressure. The margin compression matters because it reduces internal cash generation available to fund the ₹9,500+ crore gigafactory programme.

**Lithium-ion strategy — evidence:**

*Technology partnership:* Amara Raja Advanced Cell Technologies (ARACT) signed a technology licensing agreement with GIB EnergyX Slovakia (a joint venture between Gotion High-Tech and InoBat Energy) on June 24, 2024. The agreement covers LFP cell manufacturing in cylindrical and prismatic form factors. This is a licensing deal, not an equity investment in Gotion. Source: Amara Raja official press release, June 24, 2024.

*Gigafactory status:* The Amara Raja Giga Corridor is planned at Divitipalli, Telangana. Phase 1 target is approximately 4 GWh (NMC + LFP cells) with a total multi-phase target of 16 GWh. The Cell Qualification Plant (CQP) — a prerequisite pilot facility — had a groundbreaking ceremony in August 2024. Building construction for the main gigafactory commenced August 2025. Commercial cell production was originally targeted for 2025, delayed to Q2/Q3 FY27 (October–December 2026). Source: Autocar Professional, August 2025.

*OEM commitments:* ARACT signed agreements with Ather Energy (August 1, 2024) and Piaggio India (August 12, 2024) for development and supply of NMC and LFP cells for 2-wheeler and 3-wheeler EVs. These are development-stage MoUs contingent on CQP qualification and gigafactory commissioning — they are not purchase orders. Source: Business Standard, Autocar Professional, August 2024.

*Lead battery operations:* Phase 1 of a lead battery recycling plant was commissioned in December 2024. UPS battery revenue continues to grow. The aftermarket (auto service) channel remains strong.

**ASF gaps:**

Strategic Clarity: 7/10. The 16 GWh / FY30 target is clearly stated, but the Phase 1 execution pathway shows delay, suggesting planning precision is lower than strategy clarity.

Manufacturing Readiness: 5/10. No commercial Li-ion cell line exists yet. The CQP groundbreaking occurred August 2024 but production qualification typically takes 9–12 months after physical completion. This is the most critical gap.

Dependency Risk: 7/10 for LAB dependency (lower = better here). Amara Raja's dependency on Chinese technology (Gotion) introduces geopolitical risk that Exide also shares but Amara Raja has no clear fallback.

**CRT estimate for Li-ion cell manufacturing capability:** ~27 months from June 2026 to achieve commercial-scale LFP cell production and OEM sample delivery. This is consistent with management's stated FY27-end target. Confidence: 8/10 based on disclosed milestones.

### 2.2 Exide Industries

**Public identity:** India's largest lead-acid battery company by revenue. Parallel build of a lithium-ion cell gigafactory through wholly-owned subsidiary Exide Energy Solutions Limited (EESL).

**FY25 financials (standalone, as reported):**
- Revenue: ₹16,588 crore (+2.8% YoY)  
- EBITDA margin: ~11.4% (+50 bps vs FY24)  
- PAT: ₹1,077 crore  
- EESL cumulative investment: ₹4,802 crore  
- Net debt: approximately zero (as disclosed in Q4 FY25 earnings)  
- Credit rating: ICRA AAA/Stable

Source: Business Standard Q4 FY25 results; ICRA Ratings press release July 2024.

**ICRA note on downgrade trigger:** "Lithium-ion project capex leading to weakening of debt metrics on a sustained basis." Current CFO of ~₹1,298 crore provides headroom but cumulative EESL spend of ₹4,802 crore without revenue is the key credit watch item. Source: ICRA Ratings, July 2024.

**Lithium-ion strategy — evidence:**

*Technology partnership:* SVOLT Energy Technology (China), a subsidiary of Great Wall Motor. Technology covers NMC and LFP cell formats. Source: Multiple public disclosures, 2023–2025.

*Gigafactory status:* Bengaluru plant, Phase 1 capacity 6 GWh (3 GWh NMC + 3 GWh LFP). Physical plant construction complete as of FY25 end; trial production underway as of H1 FY26. Commercial cell sales are not yet occurring. Source: Autocar Professional; Benchmark Minerals Intelligence, 2024–2025.

*OEM commitment — Hyundai/Kia:* A strategic MOU was signed with Hyundai Motor Company and Kia Corporation in April 2024. All future Hyundai and Kia EV models sold in India are intended to use LFP batteries from EESL's Bengaluru plant. Source: Hyundai Global Newsroom, April 2024 (official announcement). This is the single most valuable disclosed demand anchor in the Indian battery sector — it provides a concrete volume backstop that Amara Raja's MoUs do not yet match.

*Solar:* Exide's solar revenue crossed ₹1,000 crore in FY25 — a meaningful diversification within the energy storage space.

*Antimony impact:* Antimony price surge (~5x YoY due to Chinese export restrictions) compressed Q4 FY25 EBITDA margins to ~11.2% vs 12.9% YoY. Source: Scanx.trade/Business Standard, Q4 FY25 earnings.

**ASF assessment:**

Exide is the most advanced of the three companies on the Li-ion transition trajectory because: (a) the physical plant is closer to production than Amara Raja's, (b) the Hyundai/Kia MOU provides a named, committed volume anchor, and (c) zero net debt gives balance sheet runway. Against this: ₹4,802 crore invested with no Li-ion revenue yet is a growing execution question, and commercial production has already slipped from FY25 → FY26 → FY27.

**CRT estimate:** ~18 months to deliver commercial-scale LFP cells to Hyundai/Kia and achieve first homologation. This assumes trial production exits mid-FY26 and OEM qualification takes 12–18 months. Confidence: 8/10.

### 2.3 HBL Engineering

**Public identity:** Niche specialist in industrial and defence batteries (Ni-Cad, Silver-Zinc, Lead-acid for railways), Railway Safety (Kavach/TCAS), and Defence Electronics. Second-largest nickel-cadmium battery manufacturer globally (self-disclosed).

**FY25 financials (consolidated, CARE Ratings audited):**
- Revenue: ₹1,977.59 crore (−11.7% YoY)  
- EBITDA margin: 20.35% (+116 bps — highest of three companies)  
- PAT: ₹276.48 crore (−1.4%)  
- Gearing: 0.05x (near zero debt)  
- Interest coverage: 30.91x  
- Credit rating: CARE A+/Positive

Source: CARE Ratings press release, February 25, 2026 (explicitly covering audited FY25 consolidated financials).

**Revenue decline explanation:** CARE Ratings explicitly states: "FY25 revenue was marginally lower-than-projected, due to softness in telecom segment and delay in RDSO approval." The RDSO approval delay (Kavach V4.0) was a certification gate beyond HBL's control. When V4.0 was granted on May 13, 2025, HBL was the first OEM to receive it. Source: CARE Ratings February 2026; Equentis Research May 2025.

**9-Month FY26 (April–December 2025) — remarkable recovery:**
- Revenue: ₹2,698.71 crore (+80% YoY)  
- EBITDA margin: 37.23% vs 21.96% in 9MFY25  
- PAT: ₹746.07 crore (+80% YoY)  
- Interest coverage: 81.02x

Source: CARE Ratings, February 2026.

**Kavach order book — evidence:**

As of July 2025, HBL's Kavach/TCAS order book stands at ₹4,479 crore. Key contracts:

*CLW order (December 2024):* ₹1,522.40 crore for on-board TCAS equipment in 2,200 locomotives. CARE Ratings states: "HBL supplied 76% of the order book within the stipulated execution timeline" before the balance (~₹365 crore) was cancelled per order terms. This simultaneously demonstrates execution capacity (76% on time) and concentration risk (24% cancelled, revenue impact realised).

*Central Railway contract:* Awarded after V4.0 certification. Amount not publicly disclosed in reviewed sources.

Source: CARE Ratings, February 2026; Equentis Research, May 2025.

**Ni-Cad capacity constraint:**
Ni-Cad batteries (used by Indian Air Force, Indian Navy submarines, industrial UPS) are at 90% utilization. Export revenue grew 40% YoY in FY25. No specific expansion plan for Ni-Cad capacity has been disclosed in reviewed public sources. This is a potential upside limitation. Source: CARE Ratings; HBL FY25 Annual Report (summary disclosures).

**Strategic investments:**
- Tonbo Imaging: ₹86.7 crore equity stake for defence electro-optics (IED detection, thermal imaging). Disclosed January 2026.
- Cochin Shipyard JV: Announced January 2026 for naval vessel systems integration. Specific terms not publicly disclosed.
- Li-ion industrial batteries: Expanding capacity for industrial/telecom replacement applications. Not yet significant in revenue.

**ASF assessment:**

HBL's adaptation gap is the smallest of the three companies because its current capability (defence batteries, railway electronics) closely matches where it is trying to grow (more defence, more Kavach, more exports). The strategic ask is execution scale-up within an existing certified domain, not a technology leap into an unfamiliar manufacturing process.

The risk is not capability — it is concentration. The FY25 revenue decline was caused by a single regulatory approval gate. That pattern of dependence on one customer segment (railways) and one regulator (RDSO) is a structural vulnerability that 9M FY26 performance does not resolve because the recovery itself is driven by the same concentrated sources.

**CRT estimate:** ~9 months to execute the ₹4,479 crore order book to the level HBL has demonstrated in CLW (75%+ on-time delivery within contract timelines). This is an execution CRT rather than a capability-building CRT. Confidence: 9/10.

## 3. Cross-Company Comparison

### 3.1 ASF Scores Summary

| Dimension | Amara Raja | Exide | HBL Engineering |
|---|---|---|---|
| Strategic Clarity | 7 | 7 | 8 |
| Capability Creation | 6 | 7 | 7 |
| Technology Transition Readiness | 6 | 7 | 5 |
| Manufacturing Readiness | 5 | 7 | 6 |
| Execution Velocity | 5 | 6 | 8 |
| Dependency Risk (higher = more risk) | 7 | 7 | 8 |
| Adaptation Gap (higher = larger gap) | 6 | 5 | 4 |
| Evidence Confidence | 8 | 8 | 9 |

Scores are 1–10. For Dependency Risk and Adaptation Gap, higher scores indicate greater risk.

### 3.2 What the Scores Mean in Plain Language

**Amara Raja** scored identically to Exide on Strategic Clarity (7) but lower on Manufacturing Readiness (5 vs 7) and Execution Velocity (5 vs 6). This reflects the physical gap: Exide's Bengaluru plant exists and is in trial production; Amara Raja's gigafactory building construction only commenced August 2025. The strategy is clear; the execution is behind.

**Exide** scored highest on Manufacturing Readiness (7) because it has the most advanced physical asset closer to production. Its Adaptation Gap score of 5 reflects the moderate remaining distance between trial production and commercial homologated supply. ICRA AAA and zero net debt make this the most financially resilient transition.

**HBL Engineering** scored highest on Execution Velocity (8) and Evidence Confidence (9). The Execution Velocity reflects the 80% YoY revenue recovery in 9M FY26 after V4.0 certification — this is observable, measurable execution. Evidence Confidence is 9 because CARE Ratings explicitly audited and disclosed the FY25 consolidated financials with specific percentage figures, providing more granular verification than the other two.

### 3.3 Where None of the Three Has Good Data

The following items were **not publicly disclosed** in sufficient detail to score:

- Actual cell chemistry R&D capability (headcount, patent count, academic partnerships) for Amara Raja and Exide
- Specific cost-per-kWh targets or current achieved production cost at any scale
- OEM negotiated prices in any of the EV battery agreements
- Detailed breakdown of HBL's Ni-Cad customer mix or Naval contract values
- Margin breakdown between Kavach and battery segments for HBL

These gaps are reflected in Evidence Confidence scores of 8–9 rather than 10, and in the width of CRT uncertainty ranges.

## 4. Key Risks Not Visible in Financial Statements

**Chinese technology dependency (Amara Raja + Exide):** Both companies have licensed cell manufacturing technology from Chinese firms — Gotion (via GIB JV) for Amara Raja, SVOLT for Exide. India's current policy environment (PLI schemes, border tensions) may affect technology transfer continuity. This risk is not quantified in any public filing reviewed. It is a strategic risk that both boards are presumably managing through legal agreements, but those agreements are not public.

**Antimony supply chain (Amara Raja + Exide):** The 5x antimony price surge in FY25 is a recurring risk for all lead-acid battery manufacturers. China controls approximately 80% of global antimony supply. Any tightening of Chinese export controls — as occurred in FY25 — will structurally compress LAB margins for both companies. This is not a temporary commodity cycle; it is a supply chain vulnerability without a disclosed mitigation strategy in the public documents reviewed.

**RDSO single-gate dependency (HBL):** The FY25 revenue decline was caused by a single approval gate. The FY26 recovery is driven by the same approval being granted. This pattern will repeat on every major Kavach version transition. The mitigation is to diversify away from railway dependency — which HBL is attempting via exports, defence investments, and the Cochin Shipyard JV — but the pace of diversification is not yet sufficient to offset the concentration risk within the reviewed evidence horizon.

## 6. Methodology Note

This case study applies the Adaptive Systems Framework (ASF) as documented in the ASF repository at `github.com/hdannabo/adaptive-systems-framework`. The ASF scores organisations on ten dimensions that collectively predict the likelihood and timeline of capability realization.

The CRT (Capability Realization Time) formula is: `CRT = gap × T_base × friction_multiplier × learning_accelerator × confidence_adj`, with T_base for Manufacturing/Industrial domains set at 7.8 months per unit gap (v0.5 calibration). CRT estimates in this document are approximated from public milestone disclosures rather than computed from the full CRT engine inputs, as several required inputs (governance friction, specific execution friction scores) are not directly observable in public filings.

All scores in this document were derived by the analyst from primary source evidence. No AI-generated data was used to fill gaps where public evidence was unavailable.
