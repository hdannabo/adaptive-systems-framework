# ASF vs Other Frameworks

## The one-sentence differentiator

> CMMI tells you where you are.
> Balanced Scorecard tells you how you performed.
> McKinsey 7S tells you what is misaligned.
> **ASF tells you whether you are moving fast enough to hit your next target — and what to fix if you are not.**

---

## Why ASF exists alongside these frameworks

ASF does not compete with Six Sigma, TOGAF, CMMI, or Balanced Scorecard.
It answers the question none of them address:

**"Given our current organizational velocity, will we reach our objective on time?"**

This question becomes critical when:
- An organization commits to a multi-year transformation target
- A CEO announces a 2030 strategic objective
- A board asks whether an AI program will deliver its promised ROI
- A transformation has already started and is behind schedule

None of the existing frameworks were designed to answer this question.

---

## Comparison table

| Framework | Primary purpose | Measures | Does NOT measure |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Balanced Scorecard** | Track performance across four perspectives | Financial outcomes, customer satisfaction, process efficiency, learning | Whether current velocity will hit future targets |
| **Six Sigma / DMAIC** | Reduce defects in existing processes | Process variation, defect rate, root cause | Speed of adapting when the process itself needs to change |
| **CMMI** | Assess process maturity | Maturity level (1–5), process area coverage | Velocity of moving between maturity levels |
| **McKinsey 7S** | Diagnose organizational alignment | Fit across strategy, structure, systems, staff, skills, style, values | Time cost of realignment |
| **TOGAF** | Design enterprise architecture | Architecture quality, coverage, consistency | How fast the architecture can actually change in practice |
| **Change Management (Kotter, ADKAR)** | Manage organizational change | Adoption, resistance, readiness | Organizational velocity relative to target timeline |
| **OKRs** | Align and track objectives | Progress toward objectives | Whether the velocity of progress is sufficient to hit the objective |
| **ASF** | Measure adaptation velocity | Speed across six adaptation dimensions, primary bottleneck, time to close gap | What the objectives should be (takes stated objectives as given) |

## Where ASF adds the most value

### When other frameworks say "you are at risk" but cannot say why

A company can have a mature CMMI Level 4 process and still miss its 2030 target because the execution velocity is too low. CMMI does not detect this. ASF does.

### When Balanced Scorecard shows all green but the transformation is behind

A company can hit all its KPIs in a given quarter and still be on a trajectory to miss its 5-year commitment. BSC tracks the past. ASF models the forward trajectory.

### When 7S identifies misalignment but cannot prioritize the fix

McKinsey 7S shows you all the misalignments. It cannot tell you which misalignment is causing the most delay. ASF identifies the primary bottleneck and sizes the impact.

### When OKRs show progress but the rate of progress is insufficient

A company at 40% of its OKR target in month 6 of 12 looks like it is on track. If adaptation velocity is declining, it may miss. ASF detects this before the miss occurs.

## Where ASF is weaker than existing frameworks

**Compared to Six Sigma:**
Six Sigma has 50+ years of validated tooling, a clear practitioner certification path, and extensive case evidence. ASF has none of these yet. For optimizing existing processes, Six Sigma is the stronger choice.

**Compared to Balanced Scorecard:**
BSC has financial rigor and board-level credibility. ASF scores are analytical estimates. For reporting to investors and boards, BSC has more established credibility.

**Compared to CMMI:**
CMMI is certifiable and auditable. ASF scores are currently not independently verifiable. For regulated environments requiring certified assessments, CMMI is necessary.

**Compared to TOGAF:**
TOGAF has architecture-specific depth that ASF does not attempt. For architecture design, TOGAF is the appropriate tool.

## The honest positioning

ASF is strongest when the question is:

> "We have a target. We have a plan. We have investment. Why are we not moving fast enough — and what is the specific constraint?"

When that is the question, no existing framework answers it as directly as ASF.

When the question is process optimization, compliance, architecture design, or strategic alignment, use the framework designed for that question.

The frameworks are complementary. An organization could use:
- **TOGAF** to design the target architecture
- **CMMI** to assess current process maturity
- **ASF** to identify how fast they can realistically close the gap between current and target
- **Balanced Scorecard** to track progress once moving

---

## Key questions ASF answers that others do not

1. At current velocity, will we hit our 2030 target? By how much will we miss or exceed?
2. Which single constraint, if removed, would have the greatest impact on reaching the target on time?
3. How long will it take to close the capability gap given current friction levels?
4. Where are our fastest-moving peers, and what are they doing differently?
5. Is our adaptation velocity increasing or decreasing quarter over quarter?
