# ASF External Validation Protocol — v1.0

**Purpose:** Determine whether independent human analysts, using only public documents
and the ASF scoring rubric, produce consistent dimension scores and bottleneck
identifications — without access to any prior ASF analysis.

**Version:** 1.0  
**Date issued:** June 2026  
**Study type:** Blind inter-rater reliability  
**Status:** Ready for external analyst recruitment

---

## Why This Study Exists

ASF's prior inter-rater simulation (κ = 0.739, June 2026) was conducted computationally —
Analyst B was generated by the same system that produced the original scores. That result
is encouraging but does not constitute independent validation.

This protocol governs a study using **three external human analysts** who have
**not read any ASF case studies, scores, or methodology documents** before the study begins.

If two or more independent analysts consistently identify the same primary bottleneck
and produce dimension scores within ±1 of each other across five companies, ASF's
diagnostic output is reproducible by independent users. If they do not, the rubric
requires revision before ASF can be used in any advisory or commercial context.

## Section 2 — ASF Dimension Definitions

*These definitions are given verbatim to every analyst.*

### What you are measuring

An organization has stated a strategic objective and a deadline. ASF measures whether
the organization is moving fast enough to reach that objective before the deadline.
You are not evaluating whether the strategy is correct. You are evaluating the speed
of the adaptive process.

**The six dimensions:**

---

### Dimension 1: Observation Latency

**Definition:** How quickly does the organization detect signals that a problem exists
or that a change in strategy or operations is needed?

This includes: monitoring systems, reporting cycles, the time between a problem
occurring and leadership becoming aware of it, data freshness, incident detection
speed, and whether the organization is proactive or reactive.

**Scale:**

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1 | Real-time or near-real-time detection. Automated alerting. Leadership aware of problems within hours. |
| 2 | Fast detection. Problems surface within days. Proactive monitoring in place. Leadership ahead of market. |
| 3 | Weekly or monthly review cycles. Detection functional but delayed. Some post-hoc discovery. |
| 4 | Monthly or quarterly detection. Problems frequently identified late. Reactive rather than proactive. |
| 5 | Quarterly or longer cycles. Problems discovered post-failure. No systematic detection. |

---

### Dimension 2: Decision Latency

**Definition:** How quickly does the organization commit to a specific action after
a problem or opportunity has been identified?

This includes: approval processes, committee reviews, governance overhead, escalation
paths, ownership clarity, time from recognized need to committed response, and whether
decisions require external authorization.

**Scale:**

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1 | Same-day or next-day decisions. Clear ownership. No committee required. |
| 2 | Decisions within days to a week. Empowered leadership. Minimal approval layers. |
| 3 | Weekly approval cycles. Some governance overhead. Multi-layer review but functional. |
| 4 | Monthly or longer approval cycles. Known problem without committed response. Multiple external sign-offs. |
| 5 | Multi-month or indeterminate approval. Committee deadlock. No clear decision owner. |

---

### Dimension 3: Execution Latency

**Definition:** How quickly does the organization implement committed actions?

This includes: deployment speed, production rate versus target, program completion
percentage versus plan, manual steps in the delivery process, legacy system constraints,
and coordination overhead that delays delivery.

**Scale:**

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1 | Same-day to next-week delivery. Highly automated. Minimal manual steps. |
| 2 | Fast delivery within weeks. Some manual coordination but not blocking. |
| 3 | Weeks to months to deliver. Some manual steps. Below plan but moving. |
| 4 | Months to deliver. Significant manual coordination. Materially behind target. |
| 5 | Program at 50% or less of target after sustained effort. Delivery nearly stopped. |

---

### Dimension 4: Feedback Delay

**Definition:** How quickly does the organization see and act on results from actions taken?

This includes: reporting cadence, measurement frequency, the granularity of outcome
data, whether the organization uses leading or lagging indicators, and how quickly
outcomes are visible after interventions.

**Scale:**

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1 | Real-time outcome visibility. Automated dashboards. Anomalies detected within hours. |
| 2 | Weekly reporting. Granular metrics. Management sees leading indicators. |
| 3 | Monthly reporting. Adequate measurement but lagged. Mix of leading and lagging. |
| 4 | Quarterly or less frequent measurement. Mostly lagging indicators. Low outcome granularity. |
| 5 | Annual or no systematic measurement. Blind to whether actions are working. |

---

### Dimension 5: Learning Velocity

**Definition:** How quickly does the organization improve its processes and capabilities
from experience?

This includes: postmortem and retrospective culture, whether the same problems recur,
knowledge documentation, systematic improvement processes, and whether there is
measurable evidence of learning across successive cycles.

**Important note:** This dimension is inverted. A higher score means FASTER learning.

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 5 | Continuous improvement culture with documented learnings applied. Same problems do not recur. Measurable improvement each quarter. |
| 4 | Regular retrospectives with most learnings applied. Occasional recurrence but exceptions. |
| 3 | Occasional improvement. Some recurrence of similar problems. Learning inconsistent. |
| 2 | Minimal learning. Same problem categories recurring. No systematic improvement process. |
| 1 | Recurring failures in identical categories. No postmortem culture. Same mistakes repeated. |

---

### Dimension 6: Dependency Index

**Definition:** How many external approvals, sequential dependencies, or coordination
steps must be completed before action can be taken?

This includes: regulatory approvals, external vendor dependencies, cross-team
coordination requirements, union agreements, multi-party sign-offs, and the
number of sequential gates in the delivery process.

**Scale:**

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1 | Fully autonomous. Minimal external dependencies. Organization can act unilaterally. |
| 2 | Few dependencies. Manageable approvals. Most actions require only internal sign-off. |
| 3 | Several approval gates. Some external dependencies but manageable. |
| 4 | Multiple required external approvals. Sequential dependencies. Each step requires waiting. |
| 5 | Every action requires multiple external approvals. Regulatory, vendor, union, and customer dependencies all present and sequential. |

## Section 3 — Scoring Rubric

### How to score

**Step 1: Identify the company's stated strategic objective.**

Find the specific, quantified goal the company has publicly committed to. Examples:
- "Restore 737 MAX production to 38 planes per month"
- "Achieve $4B in AI cost savings by 2030"
- "Double revenue to $9.5B by FY31"

If you cannot identify a specific public commitment, record this on the worksheet.

**Step 2: For each dimension, find evidence in the documents provided.**

Look for specific, dated public statements. Prefer:
- Annual reports (10-K, 20-F, integrated reports)
- Earnings call transcripts
- Investor presentations
- Regulatory filings

Avoid:
- News articles interpreting company statements
- Analyst opinions about the company
- Your prior knowledge of the company

**Step 3: Assign a score 1–5 based on the evidence you found.**

Do not assign a score without evidence. If you cannot find evidence for a dimension,
record "insufficient evidence" and assign score 3 (neutral) as the default.

**Step 4: Record your evidence.**

For each dimension score, write:
- The specific quote or metric that supports the score
- The document name and approximate location (page, section, or quarter)
- One sentence explaining why this evidence leads to this score

**Step 5: Identify the primary bottleneck.**

The primary bottleneck is the single dimension with the worst performance (highest
score for dimensions 1–4 and 6; lowest score for dimension 5).

If two dimensions are tied, select the one with more direct, quantitative evidence.

**Step 6: Record your confidence.**

For each dimension, record: High, Medium, or Low.

- **High:** Your score is supported by a specific quantitative metric (e.g., a production rate, a revenue figure, an adoption percentage)
- **Medium:** Your score is supported by qualitative language in an official disclosure
- **Low:** Your score is inferred from indirect signals or absence of information

### When to score 5 on Execution Latency

Score 5 when an organization is at 50% or less of a specific stated target after
a sustained period (12+ months) of active effort. A program that has not started is
not score 5; score 5 requires visible, documented execution failure over time.

### When to score 4 on Decision Latency

Score 4 when a known, publicly acknowledged problem exists but no committed specific
response has been announced. The key test: has the organization named the problem
and committed to a specific intervention? If yes, score 3 or lower. If the problem
is named but the intervention is not committed, score 4.

### The benefit-of-the-doubt rule

When evidence supports two adjacent scores equally (e.g., score 3 or score 4),
choose the lower friction score. Only choose the higher friction score when the
evidence clearly supports the worse interpretation.

## Section 5 — Evidence Standards

### What counts as acceptable evidence

**Tier 1 (High confidence):**
- Specific quantitative metric from an annual report, 10-K, or regulatory filing
- Named executive statement in an earnings call or investor day presentation
- Regulatory finding with specific date (e.g., NTSB, FAA, FCA, SEBI disclosure)
- Financial metric directly stating progress vs target

**Tier 2 (Medium confidence):**
- Qualitative description of a process in an annual report
- Management commentary about a challenge in an earnings call
- Investor presentation describing a program or initiative
- Press release with specific program milestone

**Tier 3 (Low confidence):**
- Absence of information (e.g., no mention of real-time monitoring = inferred score)
- Indirect inference from financial results
- Pattern across multiple quarters without explicit management attribution

### What does not count as evidence

- Analyst reports or third-party commentary about the company
- News articles (even quoting company executives, unless primary source confirmed)
- Wikipedia or database summaries
- Your prior knowledge of the company independent of the provided documents
- The company's reputation or industry position

### On inferring from absence

Some dimensions — particularly Feedback Delay and Observation Latency — may lack
explicit disclosure. When a company does not describe its monitoring systems, you
may infer from the outcomes visible in the reports. Example: if a quality problem
persisted for multiple quarters before appearing in disclosures, this implies a
feedback delay. Record this as Low confidence and explain the inference.

## Section 8 — Agreement Metrics and Acceptance Criteria

### Dimension agreement

For each of the 30 dimension scores (5 companies × 6 dimensions):

```
Agreement = |Analyst_X_score - Analyst_Y_score| ≤ 1
```

A pair of analysts agrees on a dimension if their scores differ by 0 or 1.
A difference of 2 or more is a disagreement.

**Target:** ≥ 80% of 30 dimensions within ±1 across all analyst pairs.

### ALS agreement

ALS is computed deterministically from dimension scores. If dimensions agree,
ALS will agree. Record:

```
ALS variance = |Analyst_X_ALS - Analyst_Y_ALS|
```

**Target:** ALS variance ≤ 0.50 on all five companies.

### Bottleneck agreement

The primary bottleneck is the highest-scoring non-Learning-Velocity dimension.
If two analysts identify the same bottleneck, they agree.

**Target:** ≥ 90% bottleneck agreement (≥ 13 of 15 analyst-pair-company combinations).

**Critical test:** Do all three analysts independently identify the same bottleneck
for Siemens? This is the bottleneck discrimination test. If Siemens produces a
non-Execution bottleneck from all three analysts, ASF's discrimination capability
is established.

### Cohen's Kappa

Computed on a triclass grouping:
- Class 0: Low friction (scores 1–2)
- Class 1: Neutral (score 3)
- Class 2: High friction (scores 4–5)

For Learning Velocity, classes are inverted:
- Class 2: High learning (scores 4–5)
- Class 1: Neutral (score 3)
- Class 0: Low learning (scores 1–2)

```python
from sklearn.metrics import cohen_kappa_score

# Compute per analyst pair (3 pairs: A1-A2, A1-A3, A2-A3)
# Report mean kappa across all three pairs
kappa = cohen_kappa_score(analyst_1_triclass, analyst_2_triclass)
```

**Acceptance thresholds:**

| Metric | Target | Minimum | Below minimum = action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dimension agreement (within ±1) | ≥ 80% | ≥ 70% | Revise rubric for failed dimensions |
| ALS variance | ≤ 0.50 | ≤ 1.00 | Investigate dimension source |
| Bottleneck agreement | ≥ 90% | ≥ 80% | Revise bottleneck identification criteria |
| Cohen's Kappa | κ ≥ 0.60 | κ ≥ 0.40 | Rubric requires major revision |
| Siemens bottleneck (all 3 agree) | 100% agreement | 2/3 agree | Discrimination capability not established |

## Section 9 — Failure Analysis

### Dimensions most likely to produce disagreement

Based on the prior simulation (June 2026), three specific dimension-company combinations
are the highest divergence risk:

**1. Observation Latency — any company (highest risk)**

The distinction between score 3 (monthly reviews, functional) and score 4 (monthly
reviews, documented lag) requires an analyst to judge whether the detection system
is adequate versus inadequate. This judgment depends on whether the analyst focuses
on the system's existence (score 3) or documented failures of the system (score 4).

*Rubric clarification added:* "If a quality or operational problem is confirmed
by an external investigation to have persisted multiple cycles before internal
detection, score 4. If no external investigation confirms a detection failure,
score 3."

**2. Decision Latency — Boeing specifically**

Boeing's FAA approval requirement is clear. The ambiguity is whether the approval
cycle is "functional but slow" (score 3) or "slow enough to be a meaningful constraint"
(score 4). Analysts who focus on progress will score 3; analysts who focus on
cycle time will score 4.

*Rubric clarification added:* "If the approval cycle requires weeks per increment
and the company is materially behind its target schedule, score 4 regardless of
whether progress is being made."

**3. Learning Velocity — any company with mixed signals**

An organization that is investing in improvement programs but producing recurring
defects or failures creates a Learning Velocity scoring dilemma. The investment
suggests score 3–4; the recurrence suggests score 1–2.

*Rubric clarification added:* "Score based on outcomes, not inputs. Investment
in improvement programs is not evidence of learning velocity. Measurable reduction
in recurring problem categories is evidence of learning velocity."

### Evidence ambiguity sources

**Source 1 — Selective disclosure**

Companies disclose problems they have resolved more readily than problems they
are managing. An annual report about a successful quality program may describe
the program (evidence of activity) without disclosing the failure rate (evidence
of outcome). Analysts who read the activity description will score differently
from analysts who note the absence of outcome metrics.

*Mitigation:* The Feedback Delay dimension specifically captures measurement
gaps. If outcome metrics are absent, Feedback Delay should be scored 4 or 5,
not the underlying dimension.

**Source 2 — Temporal ambiguity**

Annual reports describe the year that has passed. If a company implemented a
new system in H2, the annual report may describe both the old and new state.
Analysts scoring from such reports may disagree about which state is current.

*Mitigation:* Use the most recent quarterly earnings as the primary source
for current-state dimensions. Annual reports describe capability trajectory.

**Source 3 — Positive framing of negative results**

Companies frame poor performance positively. "We are tracking toward our target"
describes $1B of $4B achieved, which is 25% completion. An analyst reading
"tracking toward" as progress will score differently from one who reads $1B of $4B
as 75% unrealized.

*Mitigation:* The rubric specifies quantitative thresholds (50% or less of target
after 12+ months = score 5 on Execution Latency). These thresholds reduce
framing susceptibility for the most critical dimension.

### Scoring bias risks

**Halo effect:** Analysts familiar with a company's reputation may anchor scores
to the company's general performance rather than dimension-specific evidence.
A well-regarded company (Toyota, DBS) may receive lower friction scores than evidence
supports simply because the analyst associates the brand with operational excellence.

*Mitigation:* The rubric requires evidence for every score. No score without a
specific document citation is acceptable.

**Recency bias:** Recent highly-publicized events (Boeing door plug, AT&T outages)
may cause analysts to score dimensions related to those events higher than evidence
from the study documents alone supports.

*Mitigation:* Analysts are instructed to score based only on the provided documents.
Prior knowledge is explicitly excluded as an evidence source.

**Central tendency bias:** Analysts uncertain about a score may default to score 3.
This produces artificially compressed score distributions and understates agreement
(since true 1s and 5s become 3s for both analysts, appearing to agree on 3 rather
than diverge on their actual assessments).

*Mitigation:* The rubric provides explicit anchors for extreme scores (1 and 5)
with specific evidence requirements. Analysts are told score 3 is the default only
when evidence is genuinely absent.
